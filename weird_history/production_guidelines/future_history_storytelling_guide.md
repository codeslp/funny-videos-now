# Future History: Storytelling & Retention Guide

> **Purpose:** Make 8-10 minute compilation videos addictive. Apply these techniques at the compilation level (across entries) and within each individual entry.

---

## 1. The Cold Open (First 15 Seconds)

**Principle: Start at the most absurd moment, not the beginning.**

Don't open with "Today we're going to talk about..." Open with the single most ridiculous sentence from the entire video, delivered completely deadpan.

**Template:**
> [ABSURD FACT from Entry 2 or 3] + "We'll get to that. But first..." + [Slightly less absurd setup from Entry 1]

**Example for Organ Loyalty video:**
> "In 2050, a man in Ohio mailed his own kidney back to his insurance company using standard postage. But we'll get to that. First, we need to talk about the loyalty program that made it possible."

**Why it works:** Creates a **curiosity gap** — the viewer now HAS to find out why someone mailed back a kidney. They'll watch through the first entry to get there.

---

## 2. Open Loops (Never Close One Without Opening Another)

**Principle: The viewer should always be waiting for at least one payoff.**

At any given moment, there should be 1-2 unresolved questions in the viewer's mind. When you close one loop, immediately open a new one.

**Techniques:**
- **Forward Reference:** "...which would later become the most expensive lawsuit in medical history."
- **Delayed Punchline:** Set up a joke in Entry 1, pay it off in Entry 3.
- **The Aside:** "We'll talk about the pig thing in a moment."
- **Contradiction Setup:** "This was supposed to fix everything. It didn't."

**Applied to Compilation Structure:**
| Moment | Open Loop | Close Loop |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Cold open | "A man mailed his kidney back" | — |
| Entry 1 start | "Loyalty program for organs" | — |
| Entry 1 mid | "Heart-shaped hearts" | Closes "loyalty program" setup |
| Entry 1 end | — | Closes "mailed kidney" from cold open |
| Transition to Entry 2 | New absurd tease for Entry 2 | — |

---

## 3. Escalation (Each Entry Must Top the Last)

**Principle: The compilation should feel like it's accelerating, not repeating.**

Order your 3-4 entries from **interesting → absurd → unbelievable → darkly hilarious**. The viewer should think "it can't get weirder" and then it does.

**Entry Ordering Rule:**
1. **Entry 1:** The most "believable" one — grounds the viewer, establishes tone
2. **Entry 2:** Weirder, with higher stakes or bigger societal implications
3. **Entry 3:** The most absurd or emotionally surprising — the one people share
4. **Entry 4 (if included):** Short, punchy closer — the "one more thing" that leaves them wanting more

**Never:** Put the best entry first. Never let the video feel like it peaked early.

---

## 4. Pattern Interrupts (Break the Rhythm Every 90 Seconds)

**Principle: The brain habituates to patterns. Break them before boredom sets in.**

Every ~90 seconds, introduce a shift. This maps roughly to every 2-3 scenes in the timeline.

**Types of Pattern Interrupts:**
- **Visual shift:** Cut from still to hero video (or vice versa)
- **Tonal shift:** Go from dry facts to a snarky aside
- **Specificity surprise:** Zoom from big picture to absurdly specific detail ("Platinum members got priority kidneys. Also, some people wanted heart-shaped hearts.")
- **Rhetorical question:** "Which raises a question nobody had previously thought to ask."
- **Pregnant pause:** Beat of silence before a punchline
- **Scale shift:** "Seventeen people did this. One man mailed his back."

**Hero Video Placement Rule:** Space hero videos so they break up runs of stills. Never put two hero videos back-to-back. Never put more than 3 stills in a row.

---

## 5. The "But/Therefore" Rule (Kill "And Then")

**Principle: Every sentence should create tension or resolve it. Never just add information.**

**Bad (And Then):**
> "HealthFutura launched an organ program. And then people signed up. And then some people returned organs."

**Good (But/Therefore):**
> "HealthFutura launched an organ program. **But** what they didn't anticipate was that offering a 90-day return policy **therefore** meant people would actually try to return organs."

Every transition between ideas should use **"but"** (introduce conflict/surprise) or **"therefore"** (show consequence). If you catch yourself writing "and then," the story has gone flat.

---

## 6. Specificity Is Comedy (Exact Numbers > Vague Claims)

**Principle: "17 people" is funnier and more believable than "many people."**

The narration should be dense with specific, absurd details. Vague statements feel like filler. Exact details feel like journalism — which makes the comedy land harder.

**Weak:** "Some people tried to return their pig organs."
**Strong:** "Seventeen people attempted to return functioning pig kidneys for a bioprinted upgrade. One man mailed his back."

**Weak:** "The subscription was expensive."
**Strong:** "For two hundred and ninety-nine dollars a month, subscribers could pre-register for bioprinted replacement organs."

---

## 7. Transition Cards as Micro-Hooks

**Principle: Transitions aren't breaks — they're hooks for the next entry.**

The transition card between entries should NOT just say "Entry 2." It should tease the next entry with the most intriguing detail.

**Bad transition:** "NEXT: The Social Credit Score Gyms of Shanghai"
**Good transition:** "NEXT: The gym that charged you more if your friends were out of shape"

The transition text should make the viewer think "wait, what?" — that reaction buys you 30 seconds of attention into the next entry.

---

## 8. The Dry Buddy Tone (Specific to Future History)

**Principle: The narrator knows everything but never oversells.**

The narration equivalent of a raised eyebrow. Key techniques:

- **Understatement for absurd things:** "Which is one of those sentences that sounds perfectly fine until you think about it for more than about four seconds."
- **Deadpan precision for emotional things:** State the devastating fact flatly. Let the viewer supply the emotion.
- **Conversational asides:** "Right. So by 2048, there were three ways to get a new kidney."
- **The pivot:** "The marketing slogan was 'Because You Deserve Human Parts.' [beat] Anyway."
- **Never laugh at your own joke:** No "LOLs," no exclamation marks, no "can you believe it." Say the absurd thing and move on.

---

## 9. The Outro That Creates Demand

**Principle: Never end with a summary. End with a question that implies more content exists.**

Bad: "And that's the story of organ loyalty programs."
Good: "The return policy was discontinued in 2050. But by then, HealthFutura had already moved on to their next idea: a subscription service for memories. But that's a different story."

This does two things:
1. **Closes the current story** satisfyingly
2. **Opens a loop for future content** — the viewer now wants the memory subscription video

---

## Compilation Assembly Checklist

Before assembly, verify the script follows these rules:

- [ ] Cold open uses the most absurd moment from the video
- [ ] At least one open loop exists at all times
- [ ] Entries are ordered by escalating absurdity
- [ ] No more than 3 stills in a row without a hero video or visual shift
- [ ] Every transition uses "but" or "therefore," never "and then"
- [ ] All numbers are specific, not vague
- [ ] Transition cards tease with the weirdest detail, not the general topic
- [ ] Narrator never explains why something is funny
- [ ] Outro teases future content without summarizing
