## 26. The Great Weevil Trial (Saint-Julien, France, 1587)
**Description:** Townspeople in Saint-Julien formally sued weevils in ecclesiastical court for destroying their vineyards. The court appointed a real defense lawyer for the insects. For eight months, lawyers argued in a real courtroom. The weevils' attorney cited Genesis, arguing animals were created before humans and God commanded them to eat plants — so the weevils had divine entitlement to the vines. The townspeople offered a compromise: they would legally deed a specific plot of land to the weevils as long as the bugs stayed out of the vineyards. The weevil lawyer REJECTED the offer, claiming the land was "too barren for his clients' nutritional needs." The judge ordered land inspectors. The final page of the court record was literally eaten by insects.
*   **Visual Elements:** A dramatic courtroom scene with a lawyer passionately gesturing at a pile of weevils on the defendant's table. A judge looking exasperated. A land inspector examining a barren field with a magnifying glass, surrounded by weevils.
*   **Caption Summary:** A real lawyer spent 8 months defending BUGS in court — and rejected a land deal because it wasn't nice enough for his insect clients.

## 27. The Lawyer Who Got Rats Off the Hook (Autun, France, 1522)
**Description:** Rats were put on trial for destroying the barley harvest. The Bishop's vicar wanted them excommunicated. Defense lawyer Barthelemy de Chasseneuz pulled off brilliant legal maneuvers: when the rats failed to appear in court, he argued the summons couldn't possibly have reached ALL rats across the diocese. He then argued the rats couldn't safely travel to court because cats would pounce on them along the way — therefore the court must guarantee their safe passage. The case was repeatedly adjourned and eventually dismissed. Chasseneuz became one of France's most famous jurists, his career launched by successfully defending rodents.
*   **Visual Elements:** A lawyer standing in a courtroom, pointing dramatically at an empty defendant's chair. Cut to: a rat on a country road, a cat lurking behind a bush. The lawyer presenting a map showing "dangerous cat territories" between the rats' homes and the courthouse.
*   **Caption Summary:** A lawyer argued that his rat clients couldn't make it to court because of CATS. He won. He became France's most famous jurist.
*   **Citation & Link:** Wikipedia, "Barthelemy de Chasseneuz" - [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barth%C3%A9lemy_de_Chasseneuz)

## 28. The Pig Executed in Human Clothes (Falaise, France, 1386)
**Description:** A sow was put on full criminal trial with professional lawyers. The pig was convicted, and on execution day was dressed in a man's waistcoat, gloves, and a pair of drawers, then paraded through the town square. An executioner was paid ten sous and ten deniers for the job — the same rate as a human execution. The event was commemorated in a fresco on the wall of the Church of the Holy Trinity in Falaise.
*   **Visual Elements:** A pig wearing a tiny waistcoat and gloves being led through a medieval town square by a chain. Townsfolk watching in somber silence. A church painter carefully adding the pig to a mural.
*   **Caption Summary:** They dressed a pig in a man's suit, hired a professional executioner, and painted the whole thing on a church wall.

## 30. Brazilian Termites Taken to Court (Colonial Brazil, 1713)
**Description:** Franciscan friars formally sued termites for eating their furniture and food. The termites were given a defense lawyer, who argued: (1) termites, as God's creatures, had a right to sustenance, (2) the termites' industriousness put the "idle grey friars" to shame, and (3) the termites were on the land first. The court ordered a compromise: the friars had to set aside a reservation where the termites could live undisturbed. Within a month, the friars were back in court begging the judge to order the termites back to their reservation. The final page of the court records was eaten by the termites.
*   **Visual Elements:** A friar in a courtroom pointing at a wooden table with visible termite damage. A lawyer passionately defending an empty chair. A close-up of the court documents with chewed edges.

## 31. The Drunkard's Barrel Walk (Newcastle, England, 1600s)
**Description:** Convicted drunkards in Newcastle were forced to wear a barrel with holes cut for their head and arms, then paraded through town on a leash. Their faces were smeared with soot, a bell hung around their neck, and people cried "A DRUNKARD, A DRUNKARD!" as they walked. Called the "Newcastle Cloak," it was effectively wearing the very container that got you drunk.

## 32. Trial by Cake — The Corsned (Anglo-Saxon England, 900s-1200s)
**Description:** Accused criminals had to eat a piece of consecrated barley bread and cheese. If they choked, they were guilty. If they swallowed freely, they were innocent. A priest would write the Lord's Prayer on the bread and recite exorcistic formulas asking God to close the throat of the guilty. The most famous case: Godwin, Earl of Kent, allegedly choked on his corsned bread after swearing he had nothing to do with an assassination — and reportedly didn't survive the snack.
*   **Visual Elements:** A nervous man standing before a priest and judge, staring at a small piece of dry bread on a plate. He takes a bite, his eyes go wide, he starts coughing. The priest nods solemnly: "Guilty."
*   **Caption Summary:** The entire Anglo-Saxon justice system: eat this cracker. If you choke, you're guilty. If you swallow, you're free. A man once met his end from the guilty bread.
*   **Citation & Link:** Wikipedia, "Corsned" - [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corsned)

## 33. The Shame Flute for Bad Musicians (Medieval Germany)
**Description:** Bad musicians were punished with a heavy iron device called the "Schandflöte" (Shame Flute). A heavy iron contraption resembling a clarinet was shackled around the musician's neck. Their fingers were then clamped to the "keys" so it looked like they were playing. They were paraded through town while people pelted them with rotten food. The device is still on display at the Medieval Crime Museum in Rothenburg ob der Tauber, Germany.
*   **Visual Elements:** A street musician with a heavy iron "flute" shackled around his neck, fingers locked to the keys. Townsfolk throwing tomatoes. The musician looking at the camera with dead eyes.

## 34. Custom Shame Masks — One for Every Sin (17th Century Germany)
**Description:** Iron "Schandmasken" (shame masks) were custom-designed to match your specific crime. Gossips got masks with grotesquely elongated tongues. Eavesdroppers got masks with exaggerated ears and spectacles. Drunks got pig-snout masks. Vain people got rooster masks with feathers and beaks. Many masks had bells attached, or trunks that whistled when the wearer breathed, guaranteeing maximum public attention. They were locked at the back of the neck.

## 35. The Skimmington Ride — Punishing Henpecked Husbands (England/France, 1300s-1800s)
**Description:** If a husband was known to be beaten or dominated by his wife, the community forced HIM to ride backwards on a donkey through town while people banged pots and pans. Sometimes an effigy of the wife was placed behind him, beating him with a ladle. The humiliated husband held the donkey's tail instead of the reins. The word "skimmington" comes from the cheese-making ladle depicted as the wife's weapon.

## 36. The Government Prescribed More Dancing During a Dancing Plague (Strasbourg, 1518)

The government refurbished guild halls as dance floors, hired musicians, and brought in professional dancers. This spectacularly backfired — more people joined in. The council reversed course, banned all dancing and music, and ordered public penance.

## 38. Propose a New Law With a Noose Around Your Neck (Locri, Ancient Greece)
**Description:** In the ancient Greek colony of Locri, anyone who wanted to propose a new law had to do so with a rope around their neck. If the Citizens' Council rejected the law, the proposer was strangled on the spot. Demosthenes remarked the law was so effective that only ONE new law was passed in over 200 years. Locri had the most stable legal code in the ancient world because literally nobody wanted to risk suggesting a change.

## 41. England's Mandatory Wool Cap Law (England, 1571-1597)
**Description:** Parliament passed a law requiring every commoner over age 6 to wear a wool cap on Sundays and holidays. The Caps Act 1571 mandated that all non-nobles must wear "a cap of wool, thicked and dressed in England" every Sunday. The penalty was 3 shillings per day of non-compliance. The law was enacted to boost the struggling domestic wool cap industry. It was repealed in 1597 — but after 26 years of forced hat-wearing, people had actually grown fond of their caps. The law accidentally created the English flat cap tradition.
*   **Visual Elements:** A constable measuring a man's head and checking his cap material. A family of commoners all wearing matching wool caps outside a church. A hatless man being chased by an officer.
*   **Caption Summary:** England literally made it ILLEGAL to not wear a hat on Sundays. It was supposed to save the hat industry. It accidentally created a fashion trend.

Anything louder was considered a public disgrace. Hiring professional criers was so common it needed its own law.

## 45. The Last Man to Demand Trial by Combat — in 1818 (England, 1818)
**Description:** In 1818 — after the invention of the steam locomotive — murder suspect Abraham Thornton threw down his gauntlet in court and demanded trial by combat. Parliament had simply never gotten around to repealing this medieval right. The court examined the law and ruled it was still valid. The victim's brother, understandably, declined to fight the accused in hand-to-hand combat. Thornton went free. Parliament, embarrassed, abolished trial by combat in 1819.

## 48. Swedish Bathhouse: Sanctuary AND Double Punishment (Medieval Sweden)
**Description:** Medieval Swedish bathhouses were legally designated as sanctuaries where criminals could not be arrested — like churches. This created a bizarre paradox: a fugitive could hide in a bathhouse untouchable, but pickpocketing inside one was a capital offense.
