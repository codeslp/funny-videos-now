She maintained a 19-inch waist at 5'8" tall, weighed herself up to three times daily, slept wearing a face mask lined with raw veal, crushed strawberries on her face, bathed in warm olive oil, and went on 10-hour forced marches she called "walks." She also had a full gymnasium installed in her palace where she worked out in a corset starting at 6 AM every morning.

## 28. The King So Inbred His Autopsy Shocked the Doctors (Spain, 1661-1700)
**Description:** Charles II of Spain was the final product of nearly 200 years of Habsburg inbreeding -- his family tree was less a tree and more a wreath. His parents were uncle and niece, and he carried identical copies of over a quarter of his genes. The famous "Habsburg jaw" was so pronounced in Charles that he physically could not chew his food and had to swallow everything whole, causing lifelong stomach problems. He couldn't walk until age 8 and didn't speak until age 4. When he finally died at 38, his autopsy was the stuff of medical nightmares: the physician reported "his body did not contain a single drop of blood, his heart was the size of a peppercorn, his lungs corroded, his intestines rotten and gangrenous, he had a single testicle black as coal, and his head was full of water." His nickname? "El Hechizado" -- The Bewitched.

## 31. The Pharaoh Who Tried to Cancel Every God (Ancient Egypt, 1353-1336 BC)
**Description:** Pharaoh Akhenaten pulled off the ancient world's most dramatic religious cancellation. In a civilization that had worshipped hundreds of gods for millennia, he declared there was only one god -- Aten, the sun disk -- and tried to erase every other deity from existence. He sent workers to brutally chisel the names and images of other gods (especially Amun) off every temple wall in Egypt. Then, not content with just rewriting religion, he abandoned the old capital entirely and built a brand-new city from scratch in the middle of the desert called Akhetaten (modern Amarna) dedicated solely to his sun god. He also revolutionized Egyptian art, ordering that he and his family be depicted with bizarre elongated skulls, wide hips, protruding bellies, and spindly limbs -- a style so weird scholars still argue about whether it reflected a real genetic condition or was just artistic rebellion. After his death, the Egyptians erased him right back, destroying his monuments and striking his name from every king list.
**Visual Elements:**
- Workers with chisels frantically hacking the name "Amun" off massive temple walls while overseers look on, dust flying everywhere
- A gleaming brand-new city rising from empty desert, with Akhenaten gesturing proudly at it while confused courtiers look at the barren landscape
- The bizarre Amarna art style: Akhenaten depicted with an elongated head, soft body, and wide hips, alongside Nefertiti and their children, all looking strikingly alien
- A dramatic reversal: future Egyptians now chiseling Akhenaten's name off the very walls he built, erasing the eraser
**Caption Summary:** "This pharaoh canceled every god in Egypt, built an entire city for his new religion, and made everyone draw him with a weird alien body."

**Description:** Empress Theodora's origin story reads like the most improbable rags-to-riches tale in history. Then she had a spiritual awakening in Alexandria, returned to Constantinople, met the future emperor Justinian -- who was so smitten he changed the law to allow a patrician to marry an actress -- and she was crowned not as his consort, but as his equal co-ruler. During the Nika Riots of 532, when angry mobs nearly toppled the government and Justinian's advisers begged him to flee, it was Theodora who said the ancient equivalent of "we stay and fight," saving the entire empire.

## 38. The Emperor Who Was Perfectly Average Height But Got Roasted for Centuries (France, 1769-1821)
**Description:** Napoleon Bonaparte was not short. At 5 feet 6-7 inches in modern Imperial measurement, he was actually average or slightly above-average height for a Frenchman of his era. The entire myth started because of a measurement conversion error -- French inches (pouces) were longer than English inches, so when his height was recorded as "5 feet 2 inches" in French units, the English-speaking world assumed he was a tiny man. British cartoonist James Gillray then ran with it, drawing wildly popular caricatures of a pint-sized Napoleon throwing tantrums next to giant hats. The propaganda was so effective that Napoleon himself reportedly said Gillray "did more than all the armies of Europe to bring me down." The other problem? Napoleon's elite bodyguards, the Imperial Guard, had a minimum height requirement of 5'10" -- so he was always surrounded by men significantly taller than him. Over 200 years later, "Napoleon complex" is still used as a real psychological term for short-man syndrome, all because of a unit conversion error and one savage cartoonist.

- Napoleon standing at perfectly normal height next to average French citizens, then a dramatic zoom-out revealing his towering 6-foot Imperial Guards making him look tiny
- A Gillray-style caricature coming to life: a tiny Napoleon stamping his feet on a giant map while oversized bicorne hat threatens to swallow him whole

- Modern people looking up "Napoleon complex" on their phones while the ghost of Napoleon throws his hands up in frustration
**Caption Summary:** "Napoleon wasn't short -- a measurement conversion error and one savage British cartoonist fooled the entire world for 200+ years."

On June 30, 1559, Montgomery's lance struck Henry's helmet, shattered, and sent a long wooden splinter straight through the king's eye socket and into his brain. Catherine de Medici's response was swift and permanent: she banned jousting in France entirely, effectively ending the sport as a royal pastime across Europe. She also never forgave Montgomery -- 15 years later, she had him hunted down and beheaded.

## 40. The King Who Wrote the Book on Witch Hunting -- Literally (England/Scotland, 1597)
**Description:** Before he became King James I of England, James VI of Scotland was so paranoid about witches trying to murder him that he personally attended witch trials, conducted interrogations himself, and then sat down and wrote an entire book about it. "Daemonologie," published in 1597, is a three-volume philosophical guide to hunting witches, identifying demons, and understanding the dark arts -- written by a sitting king. His obsession started when a violent storm nearly sank his ship while he was sailing home with his new bride, Anne of Denmark. James became convinced that a coven of witches in North Berwick had conjured the storm to assassinate him. The resulting witch trials saw over 300 people accused of plotting his death. When skeptic Reginald Scot published a book arguing that witchcraft wasn't real, James was so offended that he ordered all copies burned.
